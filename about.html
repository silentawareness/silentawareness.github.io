Self-Harm

Remember, self-harm isn’t a su*c*de attempt or a cry for attention. Instead, it can be a way for some people to cope with overwhelming and distressing thoughts or feelings.

10% of young people self-harm

Self-harm should be taken seriously, whatever the reason behind it is.

What is self-harm?

Self-harm describes any behaviour where someone causes harm to themselves, usually as a way to help cope with difficult or distressing thoughts and feelings.

It most frequently takes the form of cutting, burning or non-lethal overdoses. However, it can also be any behaviour that causes injury - no matter how minor, or high-risk behaviours.

Basically, any behaviour that causes harm or injury to someone as a way to deal with difficult emotions can be seen as self-harm.

If you self-harm, always remember that there are a lot of people who also know what you’re going through.

Most young people reported that they started to hurt themselves around the age of 12. It’s believed that around 10-20% of young people self-harm.

Why do people self-harm?

  • difficulties at home
  • arguments or problems with friends
  • school pressure
  • bullying

  • transitions and changes, such as changing schools

Signs of self-harm

If you think a friend or relative is self-harming, look out for signs, including:

  • unexplained cuts, bruises or cigarette burns - usually on the wrists, arms, thighs and chest
  • keeping themselves fully covered at all times, even in hot weather
  • pulling out their hair
  • self-loathing and expressing a wish to punish themselves
  • speaking about not wanting to go on and wishing to end it all
  • becoming very withdrawn and not speaking to others
  • changes in eating habits or being secretive about eating
  • unusual weight loss or weight gain
  • signs of low self-esteem, such as blaming themselves for any problems or thinking they're not good enough for something
  • signs of depression, such as low moods, tearfulness or a lack of motivation or interest in anything
